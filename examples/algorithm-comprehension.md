## Literature Review

**Algorithm comprehension as a distinct cognitive activity**  
Research that explicitly treats algorithms as objects of understanding shows that they differ from ordinary program‑code comprehension.  Kather (2019) frames algorithm comprehension as the ability to grasp *both* the procedural description of a problem‑solving process and the accompanying chain of reasoning (correctness, complexity, invariants).  A grounded‑theory study of twelve students further confirms that, unlike program comprehension, algorithm comprehension requires linking a *proof* or formal argument to the concrete steps of the algorithm, creating a second “dimension” of understanding that is absent in most program‑comprehension models [Kather & Vahrenhold, 2021a].  This view is echoed by Kather & Vahrenhold (2021b), who observe that learners who successfully establish connections between code fragments and proof elements tend to report higher confidence and fewer “flawed but provably correct” solutions, suggesting that the *integration of process and reasoning* is a hallmark of adequate algorithm understanding.

**Mental‑model construction and the role of intensions**  
Program‑comprehension theory provides a useful lens for the cognitive mechanisms underlying algorithm understanding.  Storey (2005) and Guéhéneuc (n.d.) argue that developers build mental models by alternating between *extensions* (observable code structures) and *intensions* (the abstract intentions or specifications that the code fulfills).  Rajlich (2009) stresses that intensions are often missing in source code and must be reconstructed by the reader; the same reconstruction is required for algorithms, where the intension includes the proof‑level properties.  Empirical work on Boolean‑expression comprehension distinguishes two levels of understanding—*tracing* (following execution for a concrete input) and *comprehension* (generalizing behavior for all inputs) [Baron & Feitelson, 2026].  The latter, more abstract level aligns with the “proof‑link” dimension identified by Kather and is associated with longer response times but higher transferability, indicating that *depth of abstraction* is a key characteristic of “having understood the algorithm well enough”.

**Assessing the sufficiency of algorithm understanding**  
Several studies have attempted to operationalise these cognitive characteristics.  Stauffer (1962) introduced the notion of *adequate comprehension* as the point at which a learner can correctly predict the behavior of a system and explain it in their own terms; this criterion has been adapted to algorithms by measuring whether students can (i) articulate the algorithm’s invariant, (ii) justify its complexity, and (iii) map each proof step to a code fragment.  More recent measurement efforts include a dedicated algorithm‑skill instrument (Szerémi & Pásztor, 2025) and plan‑based e‑assessment frameworks that evaluate the *structure* of a student’s solution against a library of basic operations (Bey & Bensebaa, 2010‑2012).  These tools treat the *alignment* between a learner’s mental model and the canonical algorithmic plan as the primary indicator of comprehension.  Complementary work on confidence and solution time (Gubo & Végh, 2025) shows that higher self‑reported confidence correlates with better alignment scores, suggesting that *metacognitive awareness* is another observable characteristic of sufficient understanding.

**Implications for teaching and future research**  
Taken together, the literature points to three inter‑related characteristics that signal a robust grasp of an algorithm: (1) **process–proof integration**, i.e., the ability to explicitly connect each step of the algorithm with its formal justification; (2) **abstract mental‑model depth**, reflected in the capacity to reason about the algorithm’s behavior across all inputs rather than only tracing specific executions; and (3) **metacognitive confidence**, which manifests as accurate self‑assessment of one’s alignment with the canonical plan.  Instructional interventions that make these dimensions visible—e.g., interactive proof‑code linking activities (Kather & Vahrenhold, 2021b) or semantic‑wave models that oscillate between concrete examples and abstract specifications (Ritter & Standl, 2024)—have shown promise in improving both performance and self‑efficacy.  Open questions remain regarding how these characteristics interact with individual differences in logical‑mathematical intelligence (Korkmaz, 2012) and how automated tools (e.g., code‑similarity or transformer‑based algorithm identification; Chourasia et al., 2022) can reliably diagnose the three dimensions in large‑scale learning environments.  Addressing these gaps will enable more precise, theory‑driven assessments of when a learner can legitimately claim to have “understood the algorithm well enough.”
