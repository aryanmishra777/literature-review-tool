## Literature Review

**Conceptualisations of Developmental Phases**  
A core tension in life‑course scholarship is whether human development is best described by *normative, age‑graded stages* or by *transitional junctures* that cut across ages.  Herring (2021) frames the life course as a series of legally relevant stages, emphasizing the difficulty of drawing sharp boundaries between them.  By contrast, Lawless et al. (2026) show that earlier models privileged fixed age bands, whereas more recent frameworks foreground “transitional junctures, relational contexts and structural influences” on care trajectories.  Mortimer & Moen (2016) similarly argue that contemporary adulthood is fragmented into “early” and “encore” stages, reflecting precarious identity formation in a shifting labour market.  These perspectives converge on the idea that phases are socially constructed and fluid rather than immutable milestones.

**Cultural and Evolutionary Models of Stages**  
Cross‑cultural work highlights that societies construct their own “folk” life‑course models while still recognizing biologically predictable milestones.  Grove & Lancy (2018) synthesize ethnographic data from ~1,000 societies and propose a six‑stage cultural model of child development that aligns with evolutionary analyses of growth (e.g., walking, talking, puberty).  This underscores that, although the timing of transitions varies culturally, the *type* of phase (infancy, childhood, adolescence, etc.) is a near‑universal scaffold for organizing social roles and expectations.

**Subjective Perceptions and the Social Construction of Phases**  
Beyond objective age bands, scholars have examined how individuals *experience* the life course.  Barrett & Barbee (2022) introduce a “subjective life course” framework that distinguishes between self‑ versus other‑focused perceptions and past, present, or future temporal frames.  Mortimer & Moen’s (2016) “early” and “encore” adulthood exemplify how cultural narratives reshape the meaning of middle adulthood.  In the health domain, Lassi et al. (2025) delineate three cardinal health phases for women—emerging adulthood, adulthood, and late adulthood—showing how a life‑course lens can integrate reproductive, non‑communicable, and mental‑health concerns across the lifespan.

**Methodological Innovations for Mapping Phases**  
Recent methodological work provides tools for empirically charting these phases.  Bernardi, Huinink & Settersten (2019) propose the “life‑course cube,” a visual schema that captures interdependencies across time, domains, and analytical levels, enabling researchers to map how a given phase interacts with other life dimensions.  Lawless et al. (2026) employ a narrative‑review synthesis of 56 theories, models, and frameworks to compare how stages are defined (chronological bands, major transitions, cultural lenses).  Piccarreta & Studer (2019) review holistic approaches such as sequence analysis and latent Markov models, highlighting how focusing on sub‑trajectories can reveal phase‑specific patterns.  Qualitative sequential analysis of autobiographical narratives (Hollstein 2019) further uncovers the lived experience of phase transitions, distinguishing reported histories from experienced ones.

**Implications for Health, Inequality, and Policy**  
Understanding phases matters for interventions.  Hertzman (2007) outlines three generic health‑trajectory models that link early physical, socio‑emotional, and cognitive development to adult biology, suggesting phase‑targeted prevention.  Pudrovska (2014) demonstrates that socioeconomic status at age 18 predicts mortality across three life‑course stages, illustrating cumulative advantage processes that differ by phase.  Lassi et al. (2025) argue that recognizing distinct health phases for women can guide preventive care and policy across the entire lifespan.  Together, these studies show that delineating developmental phases—whether normative, transitional, cultural, or subjective—provides a scaffold for research, measurement, and policy aimed at improving human well‑being throughout the life course.
