## Literature Review

**Conformance checking in legal documents has been approached from several complementary angles, most notably formal verification, semantic‑web modelling, and natural‑language processing (NLP).**  Early work showed that legal texts can be treated as specifications amenable to model‑checking techniques.  Gorín *et al.* (2010) introduced **FormaLex**, a tool that translates normative clauses into linear‑temporal‑logic (LTL) formulas and re‑uses off‑the‑shelf LTL model checkers to detect violations.  Building on this idea, King *et al.* (2017) proposed a multi‑level governance framework that formalises “lower‑level” institutions and “higher‑level” abstractions, providing a sound and complete computational semantics for automated compliance checking.  More recent advances exploit richer temporal logics and satisfiability modulo theories (SMT).  Feng *et al.* (2023) presented a bounded satisfiability‑checking method for metric first‑order temporal logic (MFOTL), enabling early verification of legal properties directly against system requirements and delivering counter‑examples when compliance fails.  Together, these formal methods demonstrate that legal conformance can be reduced to well‑studied verification problems, offering provable guarantees and automated counter‑example generation.

**Semantic‑web technologies have been leveraged to bridge the gap between unstructured legal prose and machine‑readable normative models.**  Francesconi and Governatori (2022) defined a pattern‑based approach that encodes deontic norms as OWL‑2 class restrictions, allowing decidable reasoning over Linked Open Data (LOD) sources.  Robaldo *et al.* (2023) extended this line by comparing two RDF‑centric compliance engines and showing that direct processing of RDF‑encoded facts yields scalable performance on synthetic datasets.  Anim, Robaldo, and Wyner (2024) further enriched the stack with SHACL‑SPARQL constraints, tackling aggregate and temporal requirements that are difficult to capture with plain OWL axioms.  Zhang and El‑Gohary (2017) contributed a first‑order‑logic (FOL) representation for regulatory and design information, illustrating how semantic parsing of legal texts can be coupled with domain models (e.g., building designs) to automate rule checking.  These works collectively illustrate a trajectory from ontology‑based norm representation toward expressive, standards‑compliant validation pipelines that can interoperate with existing Linked Data ecosystems.

**NLP‑driven pipelines have become the dominant strategy for checking conformance of contemporary regulatory texts, especially the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).**  Cejas *et al.* (2023) constructed a two‑stage system that first extracts “shall” requirements from GDPR provisions and then matches them against data‑processing agreements (DPAs) using a domain‑specific glossary, achieving automated identification of missing or contradictory clauses.  El Hamdani *et al.* (2021) combined rule‑based extraction with a transformer‑based text‑to‑text model to verify privacy policies, reporting higher recall than traditional classifiers.  Libal (2021) demonstrated a lightweight annotation suite (the NAI suite) that tags GDPR article 13 elements, enabling downstream compliance checks.  More recent work by Nenubari and Sadovykh (2024) continues this trend, refining the pipeline for multilingual DPAs and integrating legal‑expert feedback loops.  Across these studies, the common finding is that **semantic‑rich NLP models, when coupled with curated legal glossaries, can substantially reduce manual effort while preserving the nuanced interpretation required by privacy law.**

**Finally, the intersection of process‑oriented conformance and contract consistency highlights the need for integrated, domain‑agnostic frameworks.**  Governatori, Milosevic, and Sadiq (2006) pioneered the alignment of business processes with contractual obligations, a theme further developed by Caballero‑Villalobos *et al.* (2026) who propose goal‑model‑based compliance checking to capture high‑level, subjective regulatory intents beyond low‑level event logs.  Khoja *et al.* (2022) introduced **ContractCheck**, an ontology‑driven tool that encodes share‑purchase agreements into structured “blocks” and translates consistency constraints into SMT queries, enabling exhaustive verification of contractual clauses.  Molina‑Jimenez *et al.* (2012) and the earlier work on legal XML reasoning (Leff, 2001) illustrate how rule engines and XML‑based representations can support contract‑level compliance.  These efforts converge on a shared insight: **effective conformance checking must combine formal verification, semantic representation, and natural‑language understanding to handle the diversity of legal artefacts—from statutes and standards to contracts and process models.**  Open challenges remain in scaling to large, evolving corpora, handling cross‑jurisdictional ambiguities, and providing explainable feedback to legal practitioners, pointing to a fertile research agenda at the nexus of AI, law, and software engineering.
