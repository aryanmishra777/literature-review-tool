## Literature Review

**What causes hallucinations in large language models? – A thematic synthesis**

Hallucination—producing statements that are fluent yet factually incorrect or unrelated to the input—has been identified as a pervasive failure mode of modern LLMs.  Early surveys of the phenomenon in natural‑language generation (NLG) already distinguished *factual* from *faithful* hallucinations and catalogued a wide range of downstream tasks where they appear (Ji et al., 2023).  More recent benchmark efforts such as HaluEval have shown that LLMs fabricate unverifiable information in roughly one‑fifth of user queries, especially when external knowledge is required (Li et al., 2023).  A systematic review of the root causes (Yin, 2024) groups them into two broad families: shortcomings in the **training pipeline** and flaws in the **generation pipeline**.  This dichotomy underlies most of the theoretical work surveyed below.

### Training‑data and pre‑training biases  

The most frequently cited source of hallucination is the *quality and distribution* of the data on which the model is trained.  Yin (2024) enumerates four typical training‑related factors—noisy or contradictory examples, insufficient coverage of factual knowledge, over‑representation of certain linguistic patterns, and the use of stochastic objectives that reward surface fluency over truthfulness.  Empirical work on memorization corroborates this view: McKenna et al. (2023) demonstrate that LLMs often retrieve verbatim sentences from their pre‑training corpora and treat them as universally true, leading to “sentence‑level memorization” hallucinations when the retrieved premise is irrelevant to the current context.  A second bias stems from *statistical regularities* in the corpus; hypotheses that are more frequent than their premises are erroneously judged as entailed, a pattern that predicts many NLI‑style hallucinations (McKenna et al., 2023).  Domain‑specific studies reinforce the data‑deficiency argument: Med‑HALT shows that medical LLMs hallucinate when confronted with concepts absent from their training set, exposing a gap between the model’s internal knowledge base and the specialized factual landscape required for safe clinical use (Pal et al., 2023).

### Generation‑process and decoding dynamics  

Even with perfect training data, the *generation* stage can introduce hallucinations.  OPERA (Huang et al., 2024) identifies an “over‑trust” phenomenon in the self‑attention matrix of multimodal LLMs: during decoding the model disproportionately attends to a few summary tokens while neglecting image tokens, causing it to fabricate visual details.  A similar decoding‑centric view is offered by the Chain‑of‑Verification (COVE) framework, which shows that without an explicit verification step LLMs readily accept their own drafts, propagating errors (Dhuliawala et al., 2024).  Woodpecker (Yin et al., 2024) further illustrates that visual instructions can bias the model toward hallucinating objects that frequently co‑occur in the prompt, even when the image does not contain them.  Temperature and deterministic decoding also matter: Omar et al. (2025) report that low‑temperature (deterministic) outputs still elaborate on adversarially injected false details, indicating that *confidence* alone does not guarantee factuality.  Across these studies, the common thread is that the model’s internal belief‑updating mechanism—whether driven by attention, token‑level probabilities, or lack of external verification—can amplify spurious correlations into full‑blown hallucinations.

### Attribution, diagnostics, and emerging benchmarks  

Understanding *why* a particular hallucination occurs has motivated attribution‑based diagnostics.  Attributive Reasoning (Chen et al., 2025) proposes tracing hallucinations to internal signal patterns and introduces a benchmark (RelQA‑Cate) that categorises eight hallucination types, enabling fine‑grained error analysis.  Multimodal benchmarks such as POPE (Li et al., 2023), THRONE (Kaul et al., 2024), HallusionBench (Guan et al., 2024), and VisDiaHalBench (Cao et al., 2024) expose object‑level and dialogue‑level hallucinations in vision‑language models, revealing that instruction design and visual context heavily influence error rates.  Unified detection efforts (Chen et al., 2024) and reference‑free methods (Li et al., 2024) demonstrate that hallucination can be identified from model uncertainty or consistency without external knowledge, suggesting that the *symptoms* of the underlying causes are detectable in the model’s own output dynamics.

### Synthesis and outlook  

Collectively, the literature points to a two‑pronged origin of hallucinations: (1) **training‑phase deficiencies**—noisy, incomplete, or statistically biased corpora that embed false priors into the model, and (2) **generation‑phase dynamics**—over‑reliance on limited attention patterns, insufficient verification, and decoding strategies that amplify spurious beliefs.  Multimodal extensions inherit these issues and add modality‑specific triggers such as visual instruction bias.  Future research is converging on *pre‑emptive* strategies (e.g., self‑familiarity checks, Zhang et al., 2024) and *post‑hoc* attribution tools that can both diagnose and mitigate hallucinations without costly retraining.  By jointly addressing data quality, model architecture, and decoding verification, the community moves toward LLMs that are not only fluent but also reliably grounded in reality.
